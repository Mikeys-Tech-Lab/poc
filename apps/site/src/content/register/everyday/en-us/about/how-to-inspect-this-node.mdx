It is hard to know what to trust when a piece of writing sounds careful and the consequences
are real.

A claim about work, AI, public services, or responsibility can shape how people
understand what is happening to them.

If the writing leaves no trail, you are left with the voice alone.

That is the moment inspection is for: following a public claim without treating
the site or repository as final proof.

It also does not ask every reader to inspect everything. A good public trace
lets someone begin quickly while leaving a deeper path open for whoever needs
or has capacity to check more.

### What you are looking for

You are not looking for perfect proof.

You are looking for whether the work leaves a trail someone can follow when the
stakes are real.

Can you see what the text is claiming?

Can you see where that claim is held?

Can you see what remains uncertain?

Can you see who would have to answer if the claim caused harm or confusion?

Those questions matter because a polished piece of writing can still hide who
decided, what was assumed, and what another person would need in order to
challenge it.

### A simple way to inspect

Pick one claim.

Then ask:

- What is this writing saying?
- Who might be affected if it is wrong?
- Where should the trail be visible?
- Does the visible surface actually support it?
- What is still hidden, limited, or unclear?

That is enough to begin. The useful threshold is not total inspection; it is
enough trail to see whether the claim can be questioned honestly.

Here, the public site is where most readers enter the work. The public
repository is available at
[github.com/Mikeys-Tech-Lab/poc](https://github.com/Mikeys-Tech-Lab/poc). It is
there as a deeper trace surface, not as a final authority.

### What the site and repository can and cannot do

The site lets most readers enter the claim. The repository can show that a piece
of writing, a guidance file, or a change history has been made public.

It can make responsibility easier to trace.

It can make it easier to see what changed, what was named, and what still needs
checking.

But it cannot make a wrong claim true.

It cannot decide the meaning for you.

It cannot prove that every claim is complete.

Inspection still needs judgment.

That is why the registers matter. Everyday helps someone enter the claim
without carrying the whole audit first. Orientation names more of the structure.
Practitioner keeps the fuller trace close to the surface.

<ReadingBeat>
  The useful question is not "is there a repository?" The useful question is
  "can I follow the trail far enough to question the claim and the
  responsibility behind it?"
</ReadingBeat>

### If something feels off

If a piece of writing says one thing and the visible trail says another, do not rush past
it.

Name the difference.

That difference may mean a mistake, an overclaim, a missing limit, or a public
surface that has not caught up with what changed.

In this practice, that kind of mismatch is not noise.

It is part of how drift becomes visible before other people have to live with
it.
