The registers are not a ladder.

They are different doorways into the same reasoning path.

Some people arrive with time to inspect everything. Some arrive in the middle of
work, stress, confusion, or urgency. Some only need enough clarity to understand
what is being claimed and whether there is a path they could follow later.

The point is not to make everyone do the same amount of research.

The point is to make the path visible enough that people can enter at different
depths, trust provisionally, inspect when needed, and challenge the work when
the trace does not hold.

## Everyday

Everyday is the nearest honest doorway.

It starts from what people can already recognize:

- answers arrive confidently
- work moves too fast
- public claims sound polished
- decisions ask for trust before their reasoning is visible

Everyday is not a judgment about the reader.

It means the page begins without asking someone to carry the full research
burden first.

## Orientation

Orientation is the bridge.

It moves from recognition into structure. It can name terms such as trace,
node, register, source boundary, and inspection when those terms help the
reader follow what is happening.

Orientation should not turn into a tour of pages.

It should help someone move from "I recognize this pressure" toward "I can see
the structure underneath it."

## Practitioner

Practitioner carries the fuller trace.

It can include sources, limits, architecture, inspection hooks, and unresolved
questions. It is for readers who want to check more of the path.

Practitioner readers are not above everyday readers.

They are doing more inspection work on the same public path.

## The shared contract

The claim should not change when the register changes.

The language, pace, and depth can change. The reasoning path, boundary, and
responsibility should remain connected.

That is how the work stays open to people who need a quick doorway and to
people who have the time or expertise to inspect it more deeply.
