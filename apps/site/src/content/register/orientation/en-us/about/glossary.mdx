This page holds the shared language of the Practice of Clarity.

You do not need to read this first.
If a term feels unclear, you can return here.

The goal is not to fix meaning once and for all.
It is to keep meaning clear enough to work with.

This page does two things:

- it names the main parts that shape the work
- it explains the smaller words you keep running into while reading or thinking

### Seeds

Seeds are the parts of the work that change slowly.

They hold core distinctions, boundaries, and posture.
They are written so they can be reused across contexts without losing their shape.

They do not respond to a specific situation.
They make it possible to respond without drifting.

### Mandate lenses

Mandate lenses bring the practice into a specific kind of work.

They take what is stable in the seeds and apply it to a context.
They are closer to real situations and can change faster.

They are not the foundation.
They are how the work becomes usable.

### Context seeder

A context seeder is how a mandate lens is brought into a working environment.

It carries selected parts of the work into that space so they can shape how reasoning happens.

Reading about a lens is not the same as using a seeder.
The seeder is what makes the practice present during the work.

### Public node

The public node is the place where the work can be inspected.

It holds the materials, their history, and how they evolve over time.

It is not required to begin.
It is there if you want to see how the work is built and changed.

### Registers

Registers are different ways of presenting the same structure.

The wording and pacing change.
The underlying meaning and sequence do not.

They are not different versions of the work.
They are different ways into the same work.

### Practice of Clarity

The Practice of Clarity is a way of working that keeps reasoning visible while things are still moving.

It makes it possible to question, revise, and share thinking before decisions settle.

It is not a tool or a framework.
It is something you do.

### Words you keep running into

#### Trace

You run into trace when someone does more than give you the answer.

They also show how they got there: what they assumed, what they saw, what they are unsure about, and what still needs to be checked.

That visible record is the trace.

Tracing is the act of making that record visible.

#### Node

You run into a node when the practice is actually being carried somewhere, not only talked about.

That could be a team, a project, a group, a translation, or another place where the work is being used and the reasoning can still be followed.

What makes it a node is not the format. It is that the work is being carried there with visible reasoning and clear limits.

A public node is what you get when that work becomes inspectable on a public surface, such as a repository.

#### Bridge

You run into a bridge when two readings, claims, or nodes need to stay connected even though they do not fully agree.

A bridge helps make the difference visible, shows what is shared, and points toward the next check.

It does not force agreement. It keeps connection possible.

#### Assumption

You run into an assumption when a claim depends on something being taken as true, even if nobody has said it directly.

Assumptions are part of thinking.

The work is to name them before they quietly decide everything.

#### Boundary

You run into a boundary when a piece of writing or reasoning says what it does not cover, what it did not check, or where its claim stops.

Boundaries keep a text from pretending to be larger or more certain than it really is.

#### Interpretation

You run into interpretation when the same words start landing differently in different readers, settings, or uses.

That is normal.

What matters is noticing when the meaning has shifted enough that it needs to be named.

#### Lineage

You run into lineage when you ask where an idea, term, or method came from, and what changed as it moved.

Lineage helps keep both origin and change visible.

It lets the work travel without pretending it appeared from nowhere.

#### Drift

You run into drift when the language of the practice stays in place but the care underneath it starts thinning out.

That can look like using the word trace without showing any path, or talking about clarity without naming assumptions, limits, or uncertainty.

Drift is part of how work changes. The point is to notice it before it hardens.
