Work in modern technology now produces answers very quickly.

A document can appear in minutes.  
A plan can be generated almost instantly.  
A design option can be proposed before a conversation even finishes.

This speed often feels like progress.

But something important has not sped up at the same rate: understanding.

<ShareButton />

Understanding takes time. People still need to ask questions, examine assumptions, and see how a decision fits with everything else that already exists.

When answers appear faster than teams can understand them, familiar problems begin to show up.

Different people interpret “the plan” in different ways.  
Decisions get made using partial information.  
Work has to be redone later.  
People feel overloaded even though the tools promise efficiency.

The problem is rarely effort. Most teams are working hard and trying to do the right thing.

The problem is coordination under speed.

AI tools make this tension even stronger.

They can generate convincing explanations, designs, and code very quickly. But these systems sometimes invent parts of the reasoning behind those answers while still sounding confident.

When teams accept those answers without examining the reasoning behind them, the work begins to drift.

Progress becomes measured by how much output appears rather than how well people understand what they are doing.

Organizations often respond by adding more documentation, more meetings, or more reporting. But those reactions rarely solve the real issue.

The deeper problem is simpler.

Answers are appearing faster than people can integrate them.

This is the moment where the Practice of Clarity begins.  
It starts with a basic idea: make reasoning visible again while work is still happening.
