If the practice is useful, it will travel across contexts.

The moment it travels, interpretation begins. Under delivery pressure, this accelerates. Teams reuse language to move quickly. Meaning compresses while the surface remains.

So the question is no longer only what the practice is.

The question becomes: how does the practice stay legible when more than one node is using it?

<ReadingBeat>
  The problem is not movement. The problem is drift that becomes invisible while
  the language still looks familiar.
</ReadingBeat>

### Nodes

A node is a place where the practice is carried in real work.

Not described. Not advertised. Carried.

A team, a project, or a community. A node is defined by what it does under pressure, not by how it describes itself.

### Bridges

When more than one node exists, comparison becomes possible.

A bridge makes it possible to connect or contrast how the practice is applied across nodes.

It may be:

- a shared artifact
- a side-by-side comparison
- a trace of how two nodes approached the same problem

The purpose is not agreement. It is orientation.

### Drift

Drift is not a failure by itself. It is what happens when wording travels faster than the writings and traces that ground it.

Drift begins when:

- language is copied without the conditions that gave it meaning
- examples are repeated without their constraints
- outputs are shared without the reasoning that produced them

The problem is not that drift exists, but when it becomes invisible.

### Working with drift

Drift cannot be eliminated. It can only be made visible.

That shifts the response:

- not enforcement
- not correction from a center
- but clarification through visible traces and texts

Instead of arguing definitions, nodes can show what they did, what they assumed, and what remains unresolved.
